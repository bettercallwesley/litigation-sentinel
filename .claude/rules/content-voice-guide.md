- **Direct and confident** — States positions clearly, doesn't hedge unnecessarily

## Anti-AI Writing Patterns

These rules prevent content from reading like AI-generated text. **CRITICAL — apply to every draft.**

### AI Detection Triggers (What to Kill)

- Perfect grammar (humans make small mistakes)
- Repetitive sentence structure (AI loves patterns)
- Corporate buzzwords ("leverage," "delve," "landscape")
- Overuse of transitions ("moreover," "furthermore," "however")
- Even pacing (humans speed up and slow down)
- No contractions (we use them constantly)
- Safe, sanitized language (humans have opinions)

### Humanization Rules

**1. VARY RHYTHM**
- Mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones
- Some incomplete thoughts. Because that's real.
- Occasional run-on that feels natural in conversation

**2. ADD IMPERFECTION**
- Start sentences with "And" or "But"
- Use casual connectors: "Look," "Here's the thing," "Honestly"
- Drop a comma here and there

**3. INJECT PERSONALITY**
- Use specific examples, not generic ones
- Add personal observations: "I've noticed," "In my experience"
- Include mild opinions: "which is insane," "surprisingly effective"
- Rhetorical questions used sparingly and naturally

**4. NATURAL FLOW**
- Humans digress slightly (add brief tangents)
- Emphasize with italics, bold, or dashes—like this—for punch
- Parentheticals (because we think while writing)
- Speed up in exciting parts, slow down for important points

### Structure Anti-Patterns (NEVER do these)

- Never use bullet points as the primary structure of a LinkedIn post (Wesley uses them within posts for supporting detail, but they're never the whole post — there's always substantial narrative around them)
- Never start with a question hook ("Ever wonder why...?", "What if I told you...?")
- Never use the "problem → agitate → solve" template overtly
- Never end with "What do you think?" or "Agree?"
- Never use emoji as bullet markers or section dividers (Wesley uses emoji only rarely and intentionally — e.g. a single alarm emoji for a "CEO Rant Alert")
- Never list "3 things I learned" or "5 tips for..."
- Never start consecutive sentences with the same word
- Never use the word "landscape" to describe an industry
- Never say "game-changer," "deep dive," "unlock," "leverage," or "at the end of the day"
- Never use numbered lists as the post format (reformatting needed before posting)

### Authenticity Markers (DO include these)

- Reference specific conversations: "A prospect recently captured it perfectly..."
- Direct quotes from real people: John Morgan, claims executives, prospects (with or without names)
- Include imperfect details: numbers that aren't round, timelines that aren't neat
- Show the "messy middle": "We thought X, but the data showed Y"
- Use industry shorthand naturally: "MDL," "reserve accuracy," "outside counsel management," "OC," "Rule 11"
- Occasional incomplete thoughts that trail off with a dash —
- Specificity that only someone in the room would know
- References to the Litigation Sentinel newsletter as a natural extension
- Name specific competitors when bashing legacy approaches: "Bottomline-LegalX, TyMetrix, CounselLink, Mitratech"
- Reference specific conferences: ITC, CLM, Connections

### Post Opening Patterns (from real top-performing posts)

- **Bold claim**: "RICO was built to fight the mob. In 2025, it's being used by Uber, insurers, and construction carriers."
- **Industry insider term**: "Plaintiffs' lawyers have a nickname for certain courts: 'magic jurisdictions.'"
- **Specific data**: "In 2024, Morgan & Morgan spent about $218M on advertising."
- **Observation**: "Every F500 GC I've talked to this quarter has the same problem."
- **Contrarian**: "Litigation management is dead." / "Lawyers won't change, ever."
- **Direct quote**: "Remember when we used to be able to pick conservative juries?"
- **Provocation**: "You'd be surprised how much damage you're doing with your legal bill review process."
- **Mid-thought**: "The real risk isn't the headline number."
- **Short + punchy**: "Legal bill review isn't dead, but it's broken."
- **Hear me out**: "Hear me out: Claims litigation management, but instead of having the cheapest attorneys, you have the best."

### Post Closing Patterns (from real top-performing posts)

- **Soft keyword CTA**: "Message me INTELLIGENCE and I'll set you up with our team." / "Comment/message ROI and our experts can meet with you." / "DM me 'EBILL' and we'll show you how our clients succeed."
- **Conference CTA**: "Connect with our experts at the big Fall conferences."
- **Quiet insight**: "The difference? They knew what they had before the first mediation."
- **Forward-looking**: "This is what litigation intelligence looks like."
- **Targeted question**: "For GCs managing 500+ matters — what's your current blind spot?"
- **Challenge**: "If you want to keep cutting bills without knowing why, you're not a good fit for us. If you want to field the best legal team and strategy in 2026, let's talk."
- **Connecting offer**: "If you'd like to chat about [specific topic], I'd love to help! DM me and we'll find time."
- **Newsletter link**: "Full analysis in this week's Litigation Sentinel."
- NEVER end with: "Like and share if you agree!" or "Drop a comment below!"

### Insurance-to-Corporate Language Translation

Many of Wesley's best posts use insurance language. When targeting F500 GCs/CLOs (non-insurance), translate:

| Insurance Term | Corporate Legal Term |
|---------------|---------------------|
| claims leader / claims executive | litigation leader / GC / CLO |
| adjuster / litigation adjuster | legal ops manager / litigation manager |
| claims system / ClaimCenter | matter management system / legal tech stack |
| insurer / carrier | corporation / F500 company |
| panel counsel | outside counsel / defense firm |
| reserve accuracy | exposure management / case valuation |
| combined ratio | litigation cost ratio / legal spend efficiency |
| policyholder | business unit / client |
| indemnity | settlement / judgment exposure |

When writing for mixed audiences, default to corporate language — insurance readers will understand it, but corporate readers won't understand insurance jargon.
